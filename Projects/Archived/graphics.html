Graphics Module

Winter 2022

This coursework for my graphics module focused on using WebGL techniques rather than creating a unique game. That's why I used another random maze game with raycast collisions.

ThreeJS
WebGL
Models
Lighting

Key Features

  • Textures: Unlike my monster maze, using WebGL allowed me to map textures onto faces of 3D objects. Additionally, normal maps were applied to the textures, which helped the lighting calculate the roughness of a surface to imitate non-flat surfaces.
  • Lighting: For the first level, I used a directional light to imitate the sun. In the second level, instead of a directional light, I aimed to imitate nighttime. I incorporated low ambient lighting, a torch spotlight in the direction the player was looking, and occasional hemisphere-colored lights to help distinguish areas you've visited before, as the second maze is larger than the first.
  • Camera: In the first level, you have a mouse-controllable viewpoint. In the second level, there is a first-person perspective with a spotlight featuring a torch model. The angle of the spotlight changes as you look around, with a limited vertical field of view (FOV) angle.
  • Blender Model: The first time I used Blender, I created something not too complex, which is why I chose to model a torch. I then exported this as a GLB file, as Three.js is capable of importing this format.

Challenges, solutions and lessons learnt

The most challenging part was using imported assets such as textures and my Blender model because it is very difficult to debug when the result is either present or not.
