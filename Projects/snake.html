Snake Algorithm

Development - March 2019

The project commenced with my recreation of the classic game Snake, with a key focus on implementing an efficient snake movement and collision system. I achieved this by utilizing a queue structure composed of points to represent the snake's body. Each movement update would add a new head point to the front of the queue while simultaneously removing the tail end point. In instances where the snake consumed food, this sequence of tail removal would skip one step, effectively lengthening the snake.

Collision detection was optimized to inspect only the square where the next head was projected to be. This process involved assessing whether this square was either out of bounds or a part of the snake's body.

Beyond manual gameplay, I was intrigued by experimenting with various algorithms for controlling the snake. One approach involved a basic yet effective strategy of systematically spanning rows of the grid, leaving one space unoccupied. While this technique yielded the best results, it was relatively slow. On the other hand, I devised an algorithm that would pathfind towards the food, circumnavigating the snake's tail, all within the shortest number of steps. This approach notably enabled the snake to swiftly reach the food and expand in length, but its susceptibility to self-trapping posed a challenge.

This project underscored the delicate balance between optimal gameplay strategies and potential pitfalls, offering insights into the complexities of algorithmic decision-making and game mechanics.
